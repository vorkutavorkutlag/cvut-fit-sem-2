
// Note, for limits going to zero, simply take the opposite order of the hierarchy of asymptotes.
// Those who grow faster at large $n$, grow much slower at small $n$ - rule

$(x^{10})'=10x^9$, in general $(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$
Likewise, $(x^{-1})'=(-1)x^{-2}$. i.e. $\frac{d}{dx}\frac{1}{x}=-\frac{1}{x^2}$

Applies also for non-natural number.
$\frac{d}{dx}x^\frac{2}{3}=\frac{1}{3} x^{-\frac{2}{3}} = \frac{1}{3\sqrt[3]{x^2}}$ 
	
$\frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$

Also, $\frac{d}{dx}(f\cdot g)(x) = ((f' \cdot g) + (f \cdot g'))(x)$

Composition: $\frac{d}{dx}(5+2x)^{10})=10(5+2x)^9 \cdot 2$

The derivative shows the rate of change of the function.

Derivative of the sum is the sum of the derivatives.

$\frac{d}{dx} \arcsin(\frac{1}{x}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\frac{1}{x^2}}} \cdot -\frac{1}{x^2} = - \frac{1}{x^2 \sqrt{1-\frac{1}{x^2}}}$ 
$\frac{d}{dx} \arctan(\frac{1}{x}) = \frac{1}{1+\frac{1}{x^2}} \cdot \frac{1}{x}$

$\frac{d}{dx} \sin(\sqrt{1+x^2})=\cos(\sqrt{1+x^2}) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot (1+x^2)^{-\frac{1}{2}} \cdot 2x = \frac{\cos(\sqrt{1+x^2})}{\sqrt{1+x^2}}$
